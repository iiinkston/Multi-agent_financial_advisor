# UK Equity Market Structure

## Overview

The United Kingdom equity market is Europe's second-largest equity market, characterized by strong international orientation, commodity exposure, and financial services dominance. The market has undergone significant transformation post-Brexit, with increased focus on domestic policies and global trade relationships.

## Market Capitalization and Index Construction

### Primary Indices

- **FTSE 100**: Market-capitalization weighted index of 100 largest UK-listed companies, representing ~80% of UK market cap
- **FTSE 250**: Mid-cap index of 250 companies, more domestically focused than FTSE 100
- **FTSE All-Share**: Comprehensive index covering ~600 companies across all market segments

### Market Capitalization Characteristics

- Total UK equity market capitalization: ~£3.5 trillion (as of 2024)
- Large-cap (>£5B): ~75% of market cap
- Mid-cap (£500M-£5B): ~20% of market cap
- Small-cap (<£500M): ~5% of market cap

## Macro Drivers

### Monetary Policy

The Bank of England (BoE) sets monetary policy with significant impact on UK equities:

- **Base Rate**: Direct impact on borrowing costs, currency valuation, and international competitiveness
- **Quantitative Easing**: Asset purchase programs support liquidity and risk asset prices
- **Inflation Targeting**: 2% CPI target influences policy decisions and market expectations

### Fiscal Policy

- **Government Spending**: Infrastructure investment, healthcare (NHS), and social programs drive sector performance
- **Tax Policy**: Corporation tax rates, dividend tax treatment, and capital gains tax affect investor behavior
- **Brexit Impact**: Trade relationships, regulatory divergence, and currency volatility post-2020

### Economic Indicators

Key macroeconomic factors:

- **GDP Growth**: Post-Brexit growth averaging ~1-2% annually, below pre-2016 trends
- **Unemployment**: Generally low (~3-4%), supporting consumer spending
- **Inflation**: Elevated post-pandemic, influenced by energy prices and supply chain disruptions
- **Currency (GBP)**: Sterling volatility affects international earnings and foreign investment flows

## Sector Composition

The UK market is characterized by:

1. **Financials** (~18% of FTSE 100): Major banks (HSBC, Barclays, Lloyds), insurance companies, and asset managers
2. **Consumer Goods** (~15%): Global brands (Unilever, Diageo, Reckitt), strong international exposure
3. **Energy** (~12%): BP, Shell represent significant oil & gas exposure, sensitive to commodity prices
4. **Healthcare** (~10%): GSK, AstraZeneca, strong pharmaceutical presence
5. **Industrials** (~9%): Engineering, aerospace (Rolls-Royce), defense contractors

See `UK_sector_composition.md` for detailed sector analysis.

## Market Liquidity Characteristics

- **Average Daily Trading Volume**: ~£8-10 billion across London Stock Exchange
- **Bid-Ask Spreads**: Typically 5-15 basis points for large-cap stocks
- **Market Depth**: Good liquidity for FTSE 100, moderate for mid-caps, limited for small-caps
- **Trading Venues**: London Stock Exchange (LSE) primary venue, with alternative trading systems

## Investor Behavior Patterns

### Institutional Participation

- **Institutional Ownership**: ~75% of market cap held by institutions
- **Foreign Ownership**: ~55% of FTSE 100 owned by international investors, creating currency sensitivity
- **Pension Fund Influence**: UK defined benefit pension funds are significant long-term holders
- **Passive Growth**: Increasing ETF adoption, though lower than US market

### Retail Participation

- **Direct Ownership**: ~25% of market cap, including significant individual share ownership
- **ISA Accounts**: Tax-advantaged Individual Savings Accounts encourage retail equity investment
- **Dividend Focus**: UK market historically high dividend yield (~3-4%) attracts income-focused investors

## Regulatory Framework

- **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)**: Primary regulator for market conduct and listing requirements
- **Prudential Regulation Authority (PRA)**: Bank of England division regulating financial stability
- **UK Listing Rules**: Post-Brexit divergence from EU regulations, maintaining high standards
- **Takeover Code**: Strong protections for minority shareholders in M&A transactions

## Market Access and Trading

- **Trading Hours**: 8:00 AM - 4:30 PM GMT (regular hours)
- **Settlement**: T+2 settlement cycle
- **Currency**: GBP-denominated, with ADRs available for US investors
- **Derivatives**: LIFFE (London International Financial Futures Exchange) provides options and futures

## Key Structural Characteristics

### Strengths

- High dividend yields attractive for income investors
- Strong corporate governance and shareholder rights
- Global brands with international revenue exposure
- Deep financial services sector expertise
- Stable legal and regulatory framework

### Vulnerabilities

- Brexit-related uncertainty and trade disruption
- High foreign ownership creates currency sensitivity
- Commodity sector exposure (energy, mining) creates volatility
- Relatively low technology sector representation
- Post-Brexit regulatory divergence from EU

## Brexit Impact

### Structural Changes

- **Trade Relationships**: New trade agreements replacing EU single market access
- **Regulatory Divergence**: UK financial services regulation diverging from EU standards
- **Currency Volatility**: GBP experienced significant volatility during Brexit negotiations
- **Foreign Investment**: Some reduction in EU-based investment, offset by other international flows

### Sector-Specific Impacts

- **Financial Services**: Reduced EU passporting rights, requiring separate EU entities
- **Manufacturing**: Supply chain disruptions, customs procedures affecting trade
- **Real Estate**: Commercial property values affected by uncertainty, though recovering

## Common Questions

**Q: How does Brexit affect UK equity valuations?**
A: Brexit has created structural changes including trade disruption, regulatory divergence, currency volatility, and reduced EU market access for financial services. These factors have generally weighed on UK equity valuations relative to global peers, though the market has adapted with new trade relationships and domestic policy focus.

**Q: Why is the UK market so dividend-focused?**
A: UK companies have a strong culture of returning capital to shareholders through dividends, supported by tax-advantaged structures and investor preferences. The FTSE 100 typically yields 3-4%, significantly higher than US markets, attracting income-focused investors.

**Q: How does foreign ownership affect UK market behavior?**
A: With ~55% of FTSE 100 owned by foreign investors, the UK market is highly sensitive to currency movements (GBP strength/weakness affects foreign investor returns) and global risk sentiment. This creates higher correlation with global markets and currency-driven volatility.
