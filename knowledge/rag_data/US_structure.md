# US Equity Market Structure

## Overview

The United States equity market is the world's largest and most liquid equity market, representing approximately 40% of global market capitalization. The market is characterized by deep institutional participation, sophisticated regulatory frameworks, and strong integration with global capital flows.

## Market Capitalization and Index Construction

### Primary Indices

- **S&P 500**: Market-capitalization weighted index of 500 large-cap US companies, representing approximately 80% of US equity market value
- **NASDAQ Composite**: Technology-heavy index with over 3,000 companies, heavily weighted toward growth and innovation sectors
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average**: Price-weighted index of 30 blue-chip industrial companies, historically significant but less representative of overall market

### Market Capitalization Characteristics

- Total US equity market capitalization: ~$50 trillion (as of 2024)
- Large-cap (>$10B): ~70% of market cap
- Mid-cap ($2B-$10B): ~20% of market cap
- Small-cap (<$2B): ~10% of market cap

## Macro Drivers

### Monetary Policy

The US Federal Reserve's monetary policy is the primary driver of US equity valuations:

- **Interest Rate Impact**: Lower rates typically boost equity valuations through reduced discount rates and increased corporate borrowing capacity
- **Quantitative Easing**: Fed asset purchases increase liquidity and support risk asset prices
- **Policy Communication**: Forward guidance and dot plots significantly influence market expectations and volatility

### Fiscal Policy

- **Government Spending**: Infrastructure, defense, and social programs drive sector-specific performance
- **Tax Policy**: Corporate tax rates directly impact earnings and dividend distributions
- **Regulatory Environment**: SEC oversight, antitrust enforcement, and sector-specific regulations shape market structure

### Economic Indicators

Key macroeconomic factors influencing US equities:

- **GDP Growth**: Long-term trend growth of ~2-3% annually
- **Unemployment Rate**: Low unemployment supports consumer spending and corporate earnings
- **Inflation (CPI)**: Moderate inflation (2-3% target) supports nominal growth without eroding real returns
- **Consumer Confidence**: High confidence drives consumption, representing ~70% of US GDP

## Sector Composition

The US market is heavily weighted toward:

1. **Technology** (~28% of S&P 500): Dominated by FAANG+ companies, driven by innovation and global expansion
2. **Healthcare** (~13%): Aging demographics and biotech innovation support long-term growth
3. **Financials** (~11%): Interest rate sensitivity, regulatory environment, and credit cycles drive performance
4. **Consumer Discretionary** (~10%): Economic cycle sensitivity, e-commerce transformation
5. **Communication Services** (~8%): Media, telecom, and digital advertising convergence

See `US_sector_composition.md` for detailed sector analysis.

## Market Liquidity Characteristics

- **Average Daily Trading Volume**: ~$400-500 billion across all US exchanges
- **Bid-Ask Spreads**: Typically 1-5 basis points for large-cap stocks
- **Market Depth**: Deep order books support large institutional trades with minimal price impact
- **Trading Venues**: Multiple exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, regional) with high-speed electronic trading

## Investor Behavior Patterns

### Institutional Participation

- **Institutional Ownership**: ~80% of market cap held by institutions (pension funds, mutual funds, hedge funds, ETFs)
- **Passive vs Active**: ~50% of assets in passive index funds, creating momentum effects
- **ESG Integration**: Growing influence of environmental, social, and governance factors on investment decisions

### Retail Participation

- **Direct Ownership**: ~20% of market cap, but growing through commission-free trading platforms
- **Behavioral Patterns**: Higher turnover, momentum chasing, and social media influence
- **Options Trading**: Retail options activity has increased significantly, affecting volatility

## Regulatory Framework

- **SEC Oversight**: Comprehensive disclosure requirements, insider trading rules, market manipulation prevention
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act**: Enhanced corporate governance and financial reporting standards
- **Dodd-Frank Act**: Financial sector regulation post-2008 crisis
- **Market Structure**: Regulation NMS ensures best execution and fair access

## Market Access and Trading

- **Trading Hours**: 9:30 AM - 4:00 PM ET (regular hours), extended hours available
- **Settlement**: T+2 settlement cycle (trade date + 2 business days)
- **Foreign Access**: ADRs (American Depositary Receipts) enable foreign company listings
- **Derivatives**: Extensive options and futures markets for hedging and speculation

## Key Structural Characteristics

### Strengths

- Deep liquidity and efficient price discovery
- Strong corporate governance and transparency
- Innovation-driven economy with leading technology companies
- Stable regulatory and legal framework
- Global reserve currency status supports capital flows

### Vulnerabilities

- High valuations relative to historical norms and other markets
- Concentration risk in technology sector
- Sensitivity to Federal Reserve policy changes
- Geopolitical tensions affecting global trade
- Rising debt levels (corporate and government)

## Common Questions

**Q: How does Federal Reserve policy affect US equities?**
A: Fed policy directly impacts equity valuations through interest rates (affecting discount rates and corporate borrowing costs), quantitative easing (increasing liquidity), and forward guidance (shaping market expectations). Lower rates and accommodative policy typically support equity prices, while tightening cycles create headwinds.

**Q: Why is the US market so technology-heavy?**
A: The US market's technology concentration reflects the dominance of innovative US tech companies (Apple, Microsoft, Google, Amazon, etc.) that have achieved massive market capitalizations. These companies represent a significant portion of global technology leadership and have driven substantial market returns over the past decade.

**Q: How does institutional ownership affect market behavior?**
A: High institutional ownership (80%+) creates more stable, long-term oriented trading patterns but also increases correlation during market stress as institutions rebalance simultaneously. Passive investing has increased market efficiency but also created momentum effects and reduced price discovery for individual stocks.
