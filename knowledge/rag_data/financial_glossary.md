# Financial Glossary

## Overview

This glossary provides definitions and explanations of key financial terms used in equity market analysis, risk assessment, and portfolio construction. Each term includes a clear definition, interpretation, and context for use in financial analysis.

## Return Metrics

### Annualized Return

**Definition:** The average compound rate of return per year over a specified period, accounting for the effect of compounding.

**Calculation:** If an investment grows from $100 to $200 over 10 years, the annualized return is approximately 7.2% (using compound annual growth rate formula).

**Interpretation:** 
- Represents the average yearly return if returns were constant
- Accounts for compounding effects (reinvesting gains)
- Smooths out year-to-year volatility
- Example: 10% annualized return means $100 becomes $259 over 10 years

**Usage:** Used to compare investments over different time periods and to project long-term wealth accumulation.

---

### Total Return

**Definition:** The complete return on an investment, including both capital appreciation (price changes) and income (dividends, interest).

**Calculation:** Total Return = (Ending Value - Beginning Value + Dividends) / Beginning Value

**Interpretation:**
- Captures all sources of return, not just price appreciation
- Important for income-generating investments (high-dividend stocks, REITs)
- Example: Stock price +5%, dividend yield 3% = 8% total return

**Usage:** Essential for comparing investments with different income characteristics (e.g., growth stocks vs dividend stocks).

---

### Price Return

**Definition:** The return from price appreciation only, excluding dividends or other income.

**Interpretation:**
- Focuses solely on capital gains
- Lower than total return for dividend-paying stocks
- Example: Stock price +5%, no dividends = 5% price return

**Usage:** Useful for analyzing capital appreciation separately from income generation.

---

## Risk Metrics

### Volatility

**Definition:** A statistical measure of the dispersion of returns, typically measured as the standard deviation of returns, expressed as an annualized percentage.

**Calculation:** Standard deviation of daily/monthly returns, annualized (multiply by √252 for daily, √12 for monthly).

**Interpretation:**
- **Low Volatility (10-12%):** Defensive markets, stable returns, lower uncertainty
- **Moderate Volatility (15-18%):** Typical developed markets, balanced risk-return
- **High Volatility (20-25%):** Growth markets, emerging markets, higher uncertainty
- **Very High Volatility (25%+):** Retail-driven markets, policy-sensitive markets, extreme uncertainty

**Context:**
- Higher volatility means larger price swings (both up and down)
- Requires higher risk tolerance and longer investment horizon
- Volatility clustering: High volatility periods tend to cluster together

**Usage:** Primary measure of market risk and uncertainty. Used in risk-adjusted return calculations (Sharpe ratio) and portfolio construction.

---

### Drawdown

**Definition:** The peak-to-trough decline in investment value during a specific period, expressed as a percentage.

**Calculation:** Drawdown = (Peak Value - Trough Value) / Peak Value

**Interpretation:**
- **Minor Drawdown (<10%):** Normal market fluctuations
- **Moderate Drawdown (10-20%):** Correction territory, common in volatile markets
- **Major Drawdown (20-30%):** Bear market territory, significant capital loss
- **Severe Drawdown (30%+):** Crisis-level, requires strong risk tolerance

**Context:**
- Measures maximum loss from peak during a decline
- Important for understanding worst-case scenarios
- Recovery time varies: Can take months to years to recover from major drawdowns

**Usage:** Critical for risk assessment and understanding downside risk. Used to evaluate investment suitability for risk-averse investors.

---

### Maximum Drawdown

**Definition:** The largest peak-to-trough decline over a specified historical period, representing the worst-case loss scenario.

**Interpretation:**
- Shows the worst historical loss experience
- Important for understanding extreme risk
- Example: 50% max drawdown means investment lost half its value at worst point

**Usage:** Used to assess worst-case risk and to set risk limits in portfolio construction.

---

### Beta

**Definition:** A measure of a stock's or portfolio's sensitivity to market movements, relative to a benchmark (typically market index).

**Calculation:** Beta = Covariance(Stock Returns, Market Returns) / Variance(Market Returns)

**Interpretation:**
- **Beta = 1.0:** Moves in line with market (same sensitivity)
- **Beta > 1.0:** More volatile than market (aggressive, growth stocks)
- **Beta < 1.0:** Less volatile than market (defensive, value stocks)
- **Beta < 0:** Moves opposite to market (rare, hedging instruments)

**Context:**
- High beta stocks (1.5-2.0) amplify market moves
- Low beta stocks (0.5-0.8) provide downside protection
- Beta can change over time as company characteristics change

**Usage:** Used in portfolio construction to manage market exposure and in risk-adjusted return calculations (CAPM).

---

### Correlation

**Definition:** A statistical measure of how two investments move relative to each other, ranging from -1.0 to +1.0.

**Interpretation:**
- **+1.0:** Perfect positive correlation (move together)
- **+0.5 to +0.8:** Moderate positive correlation (tend to move together)
- **0.0:** No correlation (independent movements)
- **-0.5 to -1.0:** Negative correlation (tend to move opposite)

**Context:**
- High correlation reduces diversification benefits
- Low/negative correlation provides diversification
- Correlation can increase during market stress (correlation breakdown)

**Usage:** Essential for portfolio diversification. Lower correlation between assets reduces portfolio volatility.

---

## Risk-Adjusted Return Metrics

### Sharpe Ratio

**Definition:** A measure of risk-adjusted return, calculated as (Portfolio Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Volatility.

**Calculation:** Sharpe Ratio = (Annualized Return - Risk-Free Rate) / Annualized Volatility

**Interpretation:**
- **> 1.0:** Good risk-adjusted returns
- **0.5-1.0:** Moderate risk-adjusted returns
- **< 0.5:** Poor risk-adjusted returns (high risk, low return)
- Higher is better: More return per unit of risk

**Context:**
- Accounts for both return and risk (volatility)
- Allows comparison of investments with different risk profiles
- Risk-free rate typically 2-3% (government bond yield)

**Usage:** Primary measure for comparing risk-adjusted performance across investments and portfolios.

## Market Characteristics

### Defensive vs Growth vs Value

**Defensive:**
- **Definition:** Stocks/sectors that perform relatively well during economic downturns
- **Characteristics:** Stable earnings, high dividends, low volatility, essential services
- **Examples:** Utilities, consumer staples, healthcare, telecommunications
- **Market Conditions:** Outperform during recessions and market stress

**Growth:**
- **Definition:** Stocks with high earnings growth potential, often trading at high valuations
- **Characteristics:** High earnings growth, high P/E ratios, low/no dividends, high volatility
- **Examples:** Technology, biotechnology, innovative companies
- **Market Conditions:** Outperform during economic expansion and risk-on periods

**Value:**
- **Definition:** Stocks trading at low valuations relative to fundamentals (low P/E, P/B ratios)
- **Characteristics:** Low valuations, moderate growth, often high dividends, moderate volatility
- **Examples:** Financials, energy, industrials (cyclical)
- **Market Conditions:** Outperform when valuations matter and during economic recovery

---

### Liquidity

**Definition:** The ease with which an asset can be bought or sold without significantly affecting its price.

**Characteristics:**
- **High Liquidity:** Large trading volume, tight bid-ask spreads, minimal price impact
- **Low Liquidity:** Small trading volume, wide bid-ask spreads, significant price impact

**Market Impact:**
- Liquid markets: Efficient price discovery, lower transaction costs
- Illiquid markets: Higher transaction costs, potential for price manipulation

**Usage:** Important for large investors and for understanding market efficiency. Low liquidity can create additional risk.

---

### Market Breadth

**Definition:** A measure of how many stocks are participating in market moves (advancing vs declining stocks).

**Interpretation:**
- **Strong Breadth:** Many stocks advancing, indicating broad market strength
- **Weak Breadth:** Few stocks advancing, indicating narrow market leadership
- **Divergence:** Market index up but breadth weak = potential warning sign

**Usage:** Used to assess market health and to identify potential market tops or bottoms.

---

## Investment Styles

### Momentum

**Definition:** An investment strategy based on the tendency of assets that have performed well (poorly) to continue performing well (poorly) in the near term.

**Characteristics:**
- Buy winners, sell losers
- Based on price trends and relative strength
- Works well in trending markets
- Can reverse sharply (momentum crashes)

**Usage:** Common in growth investing and technical analysis. High momentum can indicate overvaluation.

---

### Mean Reversion

**Definition:** The tendency of asset prices to return to their historical average over time.

**Characteristics:**
- Prices that deviate significantly from average tend to revert
- Works well in range-bound markets
- Opposite of momentum
- Used in value investing

**Usage:** Value investors use mean reversion to identify undervalued assets. Assumes prices will return to fundamental value.

## Common Questions

**Q: What is the difference between volatility and risk?**
A: Volatility is a statistical measure of return dispersion (standard deviation), while risk is broader and includes: (1) Volatility (price swings), (2) Drawdown risk (capital loss), (3) Liquidity risk (ability to sell), (4) Credit risk (default), and (5) Policy risk (regulatory changes). Volatility is one component of risk, but not the only one.

**Q: How should I interpret a Sharpe ratio of 0.5?**
A: A Sharpe ratio of 0.5 indicates moderate risk-adjusted returns - the investment is generating some excess return (above risk-free rate) per unit of risk, but not exceptional. For comparison, a Sharpe ratio > 1.0 is considered good, while < 0.5 indicates poor risk-adjusted returns. The specific interpretation depends on the investment category and market conditions.

**Q: What does high correlation mean for portfolio diversification?**
A: High correlation (e.g., 0.8-1.0) means investments move together, reducing diversification benefits. If two stocks have 0.9 correlation, they provide little diversification - when one falls, the other likely falls too. For effective diversification, seek lower correlation (< 0.5) or negative correlation. However, correlation can increase during market stress, reducing diversification when most needed.

**Q: How do defensive and growth stocks differ in market downturns?**
A: Defensive stocks (utilities, consumer staples) typically outperform during market downturns due to: (1) Stable earnings (essential services), (2) High dividends (income support), and (3) Lower volatility. Growth stocks (technology, biotech) typically underperform during downturns due to: (1) High valuations (vulnerable to multiple compression), (2) Earnings sensitivity to economic cycles, and (3) High volatility amplifying declines. However, growth stocks typically outperform during recoveries and expansions.
